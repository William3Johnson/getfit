Kids' Health Challenges

Out of 85,000 registered chemicals in the U.S., the federal government has required less than 200 of these to be tested for human safety – While only five have been banned since 1990. Our babies and children are growing in a toxic environment. In addition, 80% of kids between the ages of 6 and 11 are not getting enough of the daily nutrients they need from vegetables, and nearly 75% don’t get enough fruit.

It is our responsibility to enhance their health and improve their quality of life. Our kids need high-grade, organic nutrients and quality water to keep their immune systems strong. As their nutritional gatekeepers, it’s up to us to help them avoid obesity and other nutritionally-related health challenges by providing them with stealth nutrition.

What's Affecting Our Kids?

1. About 1/3 of kids in the U.S. are overweight or obese, according to the Centers of Disease Control and Prevention.

2. One 12 oz. soda contains on average 10-12 teaspoons of sugar, more than three times the daily recommended limit for children.

3. Among children (2 to 19 year of age), 23.9 million are overweight or obese and 12.7 million are obese.

4. According to National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases, approximately 1 in 20 kids has a food allergy, which is more common than in adults.

5. Over a year’s time, a child can pack on 6 extra pounds because of high fast-food consumption.

6. 3494 meal combinations were analyzed across 34 restaurant chains. Of those, 97% do not meet the expert nutrition standards for children’s meals.

7. Compared to kids that did not eat fast-food, fast-food eaters ate an average of 187 more calories per day.

8. Processed food represents an entirely new way of eating for us as human beings. Our diets have changed more in the last 100 years than they have in the last 10,000.

9. The centers for Disease Control and Prevention now projects that in 1 in 3 U.S. children will have diabetes in their lifetime (1 in 2 among African-American and Latina girls).

10. A study of 5,200 Canadian fifth-graders found that kids with healthy diets were up to 4% less likely to fail literacy tests than other children.
